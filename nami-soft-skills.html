NAMI Website Redesign

A crash course in soft skills.

Feb. 20th, 2019

Case Study Editor: Emily Remirez

Final Prototype

Overview

This was the first design project I’ve worked on with a team that had stakes. There was a client; NAMI Central Texas.

The Problem

What was the problem? Well, the NAMI team had a really deep understanding of their site and their users, because of this they pointed out two problems. 1) The website looked dated and 2) users had trouble using the site. They even had some hard data to support that. Users would call NAMI and ask for help with the site.

The Team

  2. Kristi Polozoff
  3. Osman Seyrek

Research

What did our team do? We wanted to validate the problem so we tested the navigation of the original site with a tree test and saw an overall success rate of 60%. But why? We conducted interviews and ran card sorting sessions to find out there are 2 reasons why: 1) Users didn’t understand the names of the classes, workshops, and support groups 2) Users couldn’t find specific things they were looking for on the site.

Design

Next, we designed a new navigation structure. In card sorting we learned that users organized content by who it was intended for. So that is how we decided to organize the site’s content.

Outcome

With another team I was able to run another Tree test with the new navigation. That study is still ongoing, but as of now we have seen an overall success rate of 86%.

Lessons

What I really want to share with you are the lessons I learned throughout this DESIGN project. I’ve worked on teams before, but I make the distinction of Design project because that’s the a major difference. The nature of Design vs business is that its a creative field and in some ways much more subjective. Because of that, Soft/professional skills came into play a lot more than I expected.

  1. Logistics:

    Working on project with so many moving parts and team members requires organization.

  2. Vital Decisions:

    Realizing which decisions are life or death… or neither

  3. Team Communication:

    Asking myself, “Is this the best way to ask this question?”

Logistics

This project was a big first step for me. There was a client, a 4 person team, research, designs, studys, work files… and more and more. I pretty quickly noticed the need for organization and more effective team communication.

Here’s an example of that. Ok… yes it’s just a screenshot of a calendar event, but if you look closely there is a lot more. 1) The title tells me its a client meeting, so I should be prepared 2) There is a date and time, so I know when its happening 3) I see there is a remote meeting option and link or phone number to join, so I know if i don’t meet in person I should try to find a quiet place to join remotely.

Why is all this important? Well, in other projects i’ve done, it was just me. I never created events. Why would I? No one else was joining. I had learned to work in an unorganized way.

What’s the takeaway: It’s so easy to waste time by being confused or being unprepared, so I need to

Stay organized and always be sure to communicate relevant details with the team.

Vital Decisions

On a team it’s important to determine which decisions are vital and which aren’t. In other words; Not every decision is life or death for the project. I have a tendency to make a decision and stick to it, but I realized that having that approach to every single decision isn’t the best for two reasons: 1) Obviously, I’m not always right; 2) Not every single decision is that vital.

Here is an example. I designed the top example and I was determined that that was the only way it was going to look. Another team member designed an alternative. The bottom image, but I wouldn’t budge. Finally, at some point it clicked. This isn’t a vital decision. The other design isn’t wrong. It just wasn’t my idea. Let’s go with it.

Generally speaking, If it doesn’t directly affect our user’s goal, then it isn’t vital. If it isn’t a vital decision and the other person’s idea isn’t clearly wrong then why stress over it?

Big takeaway? Trust the team and move forward. The team has the best intentions at heart and they are just as passionate about this project as I am. Trust that they are doing what they think is best.

Trust the team and move forward.

Team Communication

Ok, I’ll admit this lesson was a hard one learned. I stressed over this alot. I think the best way to talk about this is going straight into an example.

Here is a slack message. NOTE to protect my own ego this is a fake message, but it still illustrates the point. At the start of the project I defaulted to slack for all communication. “Hey, I’m gonna use this slack thing so I can be cool and its just super easy.” What you see in the screenshot is an example of a message i sent early on. I’m asking for feedback, I sent an image of a design, and I asked for a ton of specific points and questions. The problem though is that, this is not the best way to have this conversation. After an intervention from the professor, here is what my messages turned into later in the project.

It’s a simple request to jump on a call for a feedback session. With a link included.

I thought Slack failed me in a major way, but I realized i hadn’t really asked myself if I was using it the right way.

Here is the big takeaway:

Be aware of what it is that you want to communicate to the team and the best medium to do so, not jsut the most convenient.
